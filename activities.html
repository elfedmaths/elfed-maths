The counting game

The counting game

The challenge is to count together as a class. Everybody is stood on their feet and somebody will start the game by saying one, somebody else then says two, a different person says three, and so on. Each student can only say one number and you can't go in a specific order or point to the next person. If two students say a number at the same time, then you must start again from zero.

Support: students could sit down after they say their number.

Challenge: students count up in multiples of 2 or 3.

Mathletics

Mathletics

Students count to 10 together as a class.

Stage 1: every time you say a multiple of 2, you put your right arm in the air.

Stage 2: every time you say a multiple of 3, you put your left arm in the air. (Watch out for number 6!)

Stage 3: every time you say a multiple of 5, you turn around.

By this stage, every number should have an action, apart from 1 and 7.

Support: Complete each stage individually before doing them together.

Challenge: Continue counting further from 1 to 20, 1 to 30, etc.

21

21

Students can say up to three numbers, starting with one. The first student starts and says either 1, or 1,2 or 1,2,3. The next student then continues counting, saying up to three numbers. The student who then says 21 is out of the game, and the next student in line then starts again from 1. The last student remaining is the winner.

Support: to play up to 11 instead, using the same rules, for a quicker game.

Challenge: to complete the activity in Welsh.

I'm thinking of a number

I'm thinking of a number

Think of a number between 1 and 100. The students then have five questions to try and narrow down the answer, before attempting to correctly guess the number first time. They should be yes or no questions, where students are narrowing down what the chosen number is between. For example: is it odd? Is it less than 50? Is it greater than 30? And so onâ€¦

Support: think of a number between 1 and 20.

Challenge: think of a number between 100 and 200.

Bingo

Bingo

Ask students to choose six numbers between 1 and 20, and write the numbers in the back of their book. You then ask a maths question, where the answer is the bingo number called. For example, 3x2 would mean 6 is ticked off. Play for a full house.

Support: to ask simple multiplication of two numbers to find the answers.

Challenge: to ask more challenging questions using both plus and minus - a MAT mathematician could run this activity.

Pointless Bingo

Pointless Bingo

Ask students to choose four numbers between 1 and 10, and write the numbers in the back of their book. You then ask a maths question, where the answer is the bingo number called. However, the winner is the last student left remaining, so here you are not wanting your chosen numbers to be called.

Support: to ask simple multiplication of two numbers to find the answers.

Challenge: to ask more challenging questions using both plus and minus - a MAT mathematician could run this activity.

Doubling

Doubling

Two students are selected to play the game. You call a number out, and the first person to correctly double the number and shout out the right answer gets a point. Play first to three points, and then winner stays on.

Support: only use numbers less than 10.

Challenge: use numbers bigger than 50, or include fractions and decimals.

✓ or ✗

✓ or ✗

Have two columns on the board for 'good' and 'bad' numbers. Make up a secret rule for what makes a number a 'good' or 'bad' number. For example, 'good' numbers might be multiples of 3. Students take it in turns to guess a number and you write it on either side of the board depending on your rule. The first student to guess the rule wins and can then make up their own rule to play again.

Support: stick to times times or numbers containing a certain digit.

Challenge: use rules like squares numbers, cube numbers, and prime numbers.

Oracy - Describe

Oracy - Describe

This is using the literacy option on the website. There are five different areas of mathematics, as well as a random option, which could be any word from the categories. The word appears on the board along with the accompanying Welsh translation. A student then stands at the front of the classroom, with their back to the board. The rest of the class are to describe the word on the board, without actually saying the specific word, for the student to guess. See how many they can guess correctly in 60 seconds.

Support: allow students to stand at the front in groups of two or three, to answer the questions in teams.

Challenge: ask the students to give the welsh definition from the English description.

Oracy - Define

Oracy - Define

This is using the literacy option on the website. There are five different areas of mathematics, as well as a random option, which could be any word from the categories. The word appears on the board along with the accompanying Welsh translation. Select a word at random for students to define in their books - giving an example and a non example of the key maths word. Students should then feedback to the group, sharing interesting examples and nonexamples that they think other students won"t have thought of.

Support: encourage students to share their examples and nonexamples with a partner, and refining if necessary, before feeding back to the group.

Challenge: ask a student to provide a verbal example and nonexample to a new random word without any thinking time.

Oracy - Develop

Oracy - Develop

This is using the literacy option on the website. There are five different areas of mathematics, as well as a random option, which could be any word from the categories. The word appears on the board along with the accompanying Welsh translation. Students are then to use the key word in a sentence, that is written in their books. Students should then swap books to peer assess for literacy and check the sentence is mathematically correct, before swapping back and sharing their sentences with the group.

Support: encourage students to share their sentence with a partner, and refining if necessary, before feeding back to the group.

Challenge: ask a student to create a sentence to a new random word without any thinking time.
